What is this?
LinkRbrain is an online tool intended to manipulate neuroimaging, neurocognitive, and neurogenetic data. Various operations can be performed in a few clicks, directly from our query page:
  • Visualize: map your data on a 2- or 3-D representation of the human brain
  • Compare: compare your data with our presets, and view the correlations with cognitive tasks and gene expressions in the form of a table or a graph
  • Save & load: use your account to manage your results and keep them available everywhere you go
  • Export: download your results as a PDF file


The input data may come in various types:
  • Neuroimaging: you can import neuroimaging files in the NIfTI format
  • Coordinates: write a list of MNI coordinates or import a text file containing such a list
  • Presets: use presets from our database! It contains about 21,000 entries on gene expression mapping and 300 activation patterns corresponding to well-established cognitive tasks.
But... who needs that?
Depending on your domain, LinkRbrain can help you in many ways:
  • Neurology & psychiatry: display your neuroimaging data, visualize altered patterns, link them with the corresponding cognitive tasks
  • Neuroscience: display your data, compare populations, correlate them with cognitive tasks or gene expression, test hypothesis
  • Cognition: connect cognitive tasks with each other, test hypothesis

Why this project?
For our research, we needed a tool to analyze neuroscientific data in a fast and easy way.

We wanted a platform which would allow us to quickly display our results, compare groups, correlate them with known cognitive tasks and genes surexpression, and keep a track of our work.

We then decided to build such a plateform. It is now available to the scientific community!
Where do the data come from?
We mainly used two sources for our database:
  • Genetic expressions for one humain brain, provided by the Allen Institute for Brain Science
  • Peaks of activation for 310 cognitive concepts extracted from NeuroSynth (using 5809 articles) and labeled by the text-mining tool CorTexT
